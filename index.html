Designs

Ganada Made Graphic Designer, Bert Azak, is not just graphic designing, he also works with wood, glass, and limited edition prints.

Wood Designing

Wood designs and projects come in many forms. These include frames for glass and mirrors, bentwood cedar for cradles and bentwood boxes, as well as large bentwood box base drums. Cedar may also be carved.

Glass Designs

Glass vases, bowls and mirrors are the main materials used for etched and sandblast designing. The designs are original but may also be applied to multiple materials as well, which would require third party participation.

Limited Edition Prints

Designs are created on tracing paper and refined before transferring to final draft, and then transforming into original designs. Designs may take days, or or even months to create.

About

Bert Azak has been designing for over 40 years.

  • 1973 to 1982

    A very early start

    Bert started designing in grade three at Port Edward Elementary School. A First Nations art program was introduced and this is where Bert learned to draw shapes, put those shapes together and create spirit animals. These animal shapes were the cultural identity of Bert's heritage, Nisga'a.


    Bert inspiration came at a very early age from his father who was an artist and carver. Later on in life his inspiration came from his father in-law who was also an artist and carver.

  • 1982 2000

    A new beginning at home in Gitwinksihlkw

    Bert moved to Gitwinksihlkw, his father's home community in the Nass Valley, Northern BC. He continued in his highschool education and lived with his sister Julia, but also had other siblings living in the Nass Valley. Gitlaxdamix and Gitwinksihlkw are considered home to Bert because his mother was originally from Gitlaxdamix.

    Bert became a truck driver later on in 1987 and the following year was married. Driving logging trucks and having a small family took up a lot of time... his designing took a back seat to his busy schedule. Self governance and Politics were also a part of Bert's busy schedule later on in the mid 90s. Bert worked for the local First Nations Government as a front line worker, and later in the 90s entered into the political arena.

  • Spring 2000

    Transition to Vancouver

    Bert and his family moved to Vancouver where Bert started a new career with Correction Service Canada. He spent many hours on the road and very long hours at work, where the shifts were 7 days in a row with three days off, and cycling through day, evening, and graveyard shifts. This added to the strain of not being able to be creative with his art work, but he continued to work on many new designs. Some of these designs took months to complete.

  • Sept 2006 - July 2020

    Many years in prison before retirement

    Bert spent some time away from work due to a car accident but when he returned to Corrections he was returning in a different capacity. He was a plain clothes officer in social development which led to him being headhunted for Aboriginal Initiatives, now Indigenous Initiatives at Kent Institution, a maximum security prison.

    Bert continued in this capacity when transferring to other institutions and eventually transferring to community parole. An opportunity came to work at Regional Headquarters on assignment for a year as Project Officer. This led to the though of retirement as Bert had reached a milestone in Corrections, MANAGEMENT, something he had hoped for in his dreams of becoming an entrepreneur.

The Cradle Workshop

A cradle takes approximately 6 to 8 hours to build, starting with planing, then measuring, steaming and shaped, then glued, clamped, and pegged for extra support.

Cradle start out as cedar planks that are measured and carved where specific seams are to be bent once steamed. The cradle then bent around a template while the cedar dries to maintain the bent shape. Cutting and attaching the bottom of the cradle insures that the cradle will not deform while it is drying.

  • Date: January 2021
  • Client: Nisga'a Family
